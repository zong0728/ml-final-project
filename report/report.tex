\section{Introduction}
Power-grid \emph{resilience} --- the capability of withstanding, adapting
to, and recovering from large-scale disruptions --- has become a top
priority as climate-driven extreme weather grows more frequent. The
April 2025 Pittsburgh windstorm left 140k customers without power for
24+ hours despite 400 line workers in the field; Hurricanes Beryl and
Milton each downed 3M+ Florida customers in 2024. \textbf{Proactive
(ex-ante) actions} --- pre-positioning repair crews and mobile
generators before forecasted events --- are the operational lever
utilities can pull, but they require accurate short-horizon outage
forecasts. We address two integrated tasks: (i)~24-hour and 48-hour
hourly outage forecasts for the 83 Michigan counties, and
(ii)~allocation of five 1{,}000-household mobile generators to the
counties where they will absorb the most outage on the test horizon.

We are given an hourly county-level dataset with 109
atmospheric/hydrological features over a 90-day window
(\textbf{2023-04-01 -- 2023-06-30}). Two design constraints shape the
entire pipeline. \textbf{No future weather is available at test time}:
only outage history and weather observed up to the forecast origin can
enter the model --- mirroring real deployment, where utilities likewise
lack ground-truth future weather at decision time. \textbf{The training
window ends mid-storm}: the largest event of the year (peak 86{,}313
outages on 2023-06-29) starts 2023-06-23 and runs through the end of
training, so the test horizon begins inside an unfolding storm. These
constraints directly motivate our validation design (\S\ref{sec:cv})
and feature engineering (\S\ref{sec:features}).

\section{Data Analysis}
\label{sec:eda}
The data is shape $T{=}2{,}161 \times L{=}83 \times F{=}109$, plus an
outage matrix $\mathbf{Y}\in\mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}^{T\times L}$ and a per-county
\texttt{tracked} household count.

\textbf{Sparse, heavy-tailed target.} 70.5\% of $(c,t)$ cells are zero, and
the non-zero distribution is heavy-tailed (Fig.~\ref{fig:dist}). State-wide
hourly outage ranges 105--86{,}313, with the 99.5th percentile $10\times$
the median --- a zero-inflated, log-normal-tailed regression target.

\textbf{Storm-event structure.} Using a state-wide threshold of 2{,}000
simultaneous outages with a 6-hour minimum duration (12-hour merge gap), we
detect \textbf{26 storm events} in the 90 days (Fig.~\ref{fig:state}). The
largest event (id~25, 2023-06-23 -- 06-30) peaks at 86{,}313, more than
$3\times$ the second-largest (27{,}643). \emph{Training ends inside this
event.}

\textbf{Strong autocorrelation.} The state-wide series has a clear diurnal
echo at lag~24h and a weaker weekly echo at 168h (Fig.~\ref{fig:acf}).
Lagged outage features should dominate; weather is informative but
secondary, as much of its predictive value is already encoded in lagged
outage.

\textbf{County heterogeneity.} Per-county \texttt{tracked} ranges
$\sim$5{,}000 to 920{,}000. Wayne County (FIPS 26163, Detroit) tracks
922k households but its peak hourly outage of 15{,}143 is only third
largest; smaller counties like Saginaw (97k tracked, 8.7k peak) take
disproportionately large per-capita hits in storm events
(Fig.~\ref{fig:het}). This drives our policy recommendation (\S\ref{sec:policy}).

\textbf{Top weather correlates.} The 15 features most correlated with
state-wide outage are all moisture-and-wind-related (\texttt{cape}, low-level
$u/v$ winds, precipitable water, \texttt{vstm} storm-motion magnitude). We
retain all 91 non-zero-variance features; tree models implicitly select.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Validation: Rolling-Origin CV that Mirrors the Test Setup}
\label{sec:cv}
A naive last-48h holdout falls inside the year's largest storm, so any
single-window val-RMSE is a sample of one. We replace it with
\textbf{rolling-origin cross-validation} (Fig.~\ref{fig:cv_diag}) of 6
folds spaced 72 hours apart. Each fold's val window is the forecast
horizon (24/48 hours) immediately following its fit window --- exactly
the production setup. Fit windows \emph{expand}: each fold uses all data
from 2023-04-01 to its origin, so fold~0's fit set equals the final
retrain on full training data, making its RMSE the most direct estimate
of test performance [4]. No weather inside any val window is visible to
its model. Folds span 2023-06-13 -- 06-29; \textbf{exactly one fold (fold
1) lands in the big-storm peak}. We report \emph{both} aggregate-mean and
calm-period-mean RMSE: the former captures cross-period performance
including OOD storms, the latter captures typical-day quality without one
extreme event dominating.

\subsection{Feature Engineering}
\label{sec:features}
For tree-based models we materialize a $(N\times P)$ feature matrix in
which each row is one (origin time, county, horizon-step) triplet.
Features (all leak-free, using only data up to time~$t$): outage lags
$y_{c,t-k}$ for $k\in\{1,2,3,6,12,24,48,168\}$; outage rolling mean and
std over $\{6,24,168\}$-h windows; storm indicators (24h max, 24h
non-zero count, 24h day-over-day delta); weather at~$t$ and lags
$\{3,12,24\}$h; calendar (sin/cos of hour, dow, month) at target time
$t{+}h$; horizon-step $h$; location index (ordinal for LGB/XGB, native
categorical for CatBoost). Final $P\!\approx\!389$. We also tried an
enlarged \emph{v2} set (deep lags up to 672h, state-aggregated lags,
county metadata, weather rolling means; $P\!\approx\!773$) but observed
no measurable RMSE improvement (\S\ref{sec:negative}). We considered
applying PCA to the 109 weather features but did not adopt it: tree-based
GBDT models perform implicit feature selection through information-gain
splits, so a linear orthogonal basis would discard interpretability for
no measurable gain. (We verified empirically that LightGBM's gain-based
feature importance assigns 92\% of total gain to just 23 weather features
out of 91, confirming sparse usage.)

\subsection{Model Families}
\textbf{Naive baselines}: all-zero, persistence ($\hat y_{t+h}\!=\!y_t$),
seasonal-naive 24h/168h, per-(county,hour,dow) historical mean.
\textbf{Classical TS}: per-county SARIMAX(1,0,1) (the project's second
mandatory baseline) and AutoARIMA over $(p,d,q)$.
\textbf{Tabular GBDT}: LightGBM [1] (log-target), LGB-Tweedie [5],
XGBoost [2], CatBoost [3] (native categorical FIPS), Quantile-LGB at
$\alpha\!\in\!\{0.5,0.9\}$.
\textbf{Hurdle/zero-inflated} [6]: classifier $\Pr[y\!>\!0]$ times
positive-only regressor.
\textbf{Per-county models}: 83 independent regressors (LGB/XGB/Cat).
\textbf{Sequence neural networks}: GRU, LSTM, BiLSTM, TCN, MLP, vanilla
Transformer, NLinear/DLinear [7], PatchTST [8], iTransformer [9],
N-BEATS [10], N-HiTS [11].
\textbf{Equal-weight ensembles} of the top-$k$ CV models. We avoid
\emph{learned} stacking weights because they would over-fit the
single-fold storm signal.

\subsection{Hyperparameter Search and Ensemble Selection}
We swept hyperparameters on a CMU DeltaAI cluster (NVIDIA H200), totalling
$\sim$3{,}800 \emph{(model, seed, horizon, fold)} cells: 54 LightGBM
configs ($\text{leaves}\!\in\!\{31,63,127\}$, $\text{lr}\!\in\!\{0.03,0.05,0.1\}$,
$n\!\in\!\{400,800,1500\}$, subsample $\in\{0.7,0.9\}$); 24 LGB-Tweedie
(adds variance\_power $\in\{1.2,1.5,1.8\}$); 18 XGBoost; 18 CatBoost; 12
NN architectures $\times$ 8 hp combos $\times$ 2 seeds; 8 advanced models
(AutoARIMA, Quantile-LGB, hurdle-LGB/XGB, per-county models). For
ensembling we compare four combination strategies (mean, median, trimmed
mean, inverse-RMSE-weighted) and pick the lowest-RMSE one per horizon.
Top-$k$ uses score $\text{median-RMSE}(m)\!+\!0.3\,\text{std-RMSE}(m)$
with a cap of two configs per family.

\subsection{Headline: GBDT Ensembles Beat Both Required Baselines}
Tables~\ref{tab:headline24}--\ref{tab:headline48} compare our best model
to the all-zero baseline and SARIMAX(1,0,1) under four aggregation

48h calm-period. Storm fold~1 contributes RMSE 700--900 for \emph{every}
model evaluated --- including SARIMAX, all 100+ tree configurations, and
all SOTA Transformer variants --- so its absolute contribution is large
in unit terms but small in relative terms.

\subsection{Per-Family Comparison and Per-Fold Breakdown}
Figure~\ref{fig:per_family} shows the best configuration of each model
family on the 24h horizon. On calm-period RMSE, GBDT variants
\textbf{cluster tightly between 41.5 and 43.5}; recurrent and
Transformer-style sequence models trail by 10--20 RMSE units.
This pattern reproduces [7]: on short, multivariate, autocorrelated
series, simpler inductive biases (boosted trees) outperform self-attention.
On the storm fold all families collapse to RMSE~700--900, the ranking
effectively randomised --- confirming that storm-event extrapolation is
bounded by data, not architecture.

Figure~\ref{fig:per_fold} shows each top-15 model's RMSE on every fold.
Model differences within the GBDT cluster are at most $\pm 0.5$ RMSE on
calm folds, indicating saturation; fold~0 (the rolling-CV window closest
to the test setup) is the hardest calm fold (RMSE~71 vs.\ 19--44 elsewhere).

\subsection{What Did Not Improve the Result}
\label{sec:negative}
Several priors did \emph{not} reduce CV RMSE below the GBDT cluster:
\textbf{(a) doubled feature count (v2)} was within noise of the
$\sim$390-feature baseline; \textbf{(b) direct MSE / Huber /
sample-weighted objectives} on raw outage scale degraded fold-0 calm RMSE
from 71 to 73--146, reflecting heavy-tail sensitivity to log-space
training; \textbf{(c) recursive (autoregressive) one-step rollout}
accumulated error through 24--48 iterations; \textbf{(d) every NN
architecture tried} (GRU/LSTM/BiLSTM/TCN/Transformer/NLinear/DLinear/
PatchTST/iTransformer/N-BEATS/N-HiTS) underperformed GBDT by 10+ RMSE on
calm folds. Diagnostic decomposition reveals 50\% of fold-0 per-county
RMSE concentrated in Wayne County alone (truth peak 2{,}648, predicted
peak 536), an unfolding-storm underestimation that survives every variant
tried. We interpret RMSE 41--42 as the achievable lower bound for this
dataset under the no-future-weather constraint.

\section{Analysis and Policy Recommendation}
\label{sec:policy}

\subsection{From Forecast to Action: Decision-Maker Perspective}
A point forecast $\hat y_{c,t}$ is not yet a decision. From the utility
operator's vantage point, the question is concrete: \emph{given the
predicted outage geography, where do we stage our mobile generators
\textbf{tonight} so that they will absorb the maximum number of
household-hours of outage tomorrow?} The instrument has hard physical
constraints --- each of the five mobile generators serves at most
1{,}000 households at any moment, and once deployed cannot be relocated
during the 48-h event window. The decision is taken \emph{before} the
event, with the same information-set the model uses
(history + observed weather up to forecast origin), so the recommendation
is an honest test of forecast-driven proactive action.

\subsection{Why This Set Rather than Naive Heuristics}
Four naive heuristics each fail in a way our forecast-driven greedy
addresses. \textbf{(a) Top-5 historical-peak} (Wayne, Oakland, Macomb,
Genesee, Kent) treats outage as a static county property, ignoring storm
track. \textbf{(b) Top-5 by tracked households} produces the same metro
list, ignoring per-capita risk: Saginaw (97k tracked) hit 8.7k outage in
the June storm --- 9\% of its base, far more than metros. \textbf{(c)
Top-5 by predicted 48h sum} double-counts long, low-amplitude outage; the
per-hour cap $\min(\hat y, 1000)$ in $M_s$ corrects this. \textbf{(d) One
generator per county} forbids stacking, but the greedy correctly places
multiple generators where predicted demand $\gg 1{,}000$ per hour, as the
project description permits.

\subsection{Sensitivity, Robustness, and Decision Risk}
\textbf{Selection robustness.} Re-deriving the top-5 from each ensemble
member, the first 3 counties are picked unanimously; counties 4--5 vary
marginally. Median (P50) and inverse-RMSE-weighted ensembles produce
nearly identical top-5 sets, so the recommendation is not driven by a
single overconfident model.

\textbf{Decision risk against the real outcome.} Our recommendation will
be evaluated against the actual 2023-06-30 -- 07-02 outage record.
Three sources of risk could erode ex-post served-household-hours:
(a) the unfolding storm intensifies in counties our model under-predicts
(notably Wayne, where fold-0 truth peak 2{,}648 vs.\ prediction 536
identifies a systematic gap); (b) the storm clears earlier than
expected, so 48-h staging over-commits; (c) a previously-calm county
sees a flash event invisible to a model without future-weather input.
We mitigate (a) by cross-checking the Quantile-LGB-$P_{90}$ pessimistic
forecast's top-5 list (overlap with mean-forecast top-5 is the
robustness metric reported above); (b) and (c) are residual risks
inherent to the 90-day training window and the no-future-weather
constraint.

\textbf{Limitations.} (i)~Training data are 90 days of one
spring--summer; the model has not seen winter ice storms or fall
hurricane remnants, so year-on-year retraining is essential in
deployment. (ii)~No future-weather information caps 48-h predictability
of fast convective events; integrating a NWP forecast at inference
would substantially improve both prediction and policy quality but is
outside the project framing. (iii)~The recommendation is derived from
a point forecast; for richer products (uncertainty bands, regret-aware
allocation) the Quantile-LGB-$P_{90}$ member is available in our pool.
