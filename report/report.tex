\section{Introduction}

Grid resilience has moved up the priority list as climate-driven extreme
weather grows more frequent. The April 2025 Pittsburgh windstorm left
140{,}000 customers without power for over 24 hours, and Hurricanes
Beryl and Milton each downed more than 3 million Florida customers in
2024. Accurate short-horizon outage forecasts form the foundation of proactive utility response, enabling pre-positioning of repair crews and mobile generators ahead of anticipated disruptions rather than reacting afterward.

We address two linked tasks using hourly county-level outage counts and
109 atmospheric features across 83 Michigan counties over a 90-day
window (2023-04-01 to 2023-06-30). The first is hourly outage
forecasting at 24 and 48-hour horizons. The second is allocating five
1{,}000-household generators to the counties where they can mitigate the greatest level of outages over the test horizon.

Two constraints shape the pipeline. First, no future weather information is available at test time; only outage history and weather observations up to the forecast origin are used as model inputs, reflecting real-world deployment conditions. Second, the training window ends mid-storm: the largest event of the year, peaking at 86,313 outages on 2023-06-29, begins on 2023-06-23 and continues through the end of training, so the test horizon begins within an unfolding storm. These constraints directly motivate
our validation design (\S\ref{sec:cv}) and feature engineering
(\S\ref{sec:features}).

\section{Data Analysis}
\label{sec:eda}

\paragraph{Target distribution.}
The outage matrix $\mathbf{Y}\in\mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}^{T\times L}$ is sparse and heavy-tailed, with 70.5\% of county-hour cells recording zero and the nonzero values following a log-normal-like tail (Fig.~\ref{fig:dist}). State-wide hourly outages range from 105 to 86{,}313, with the 99.5th percentile approximately ten times the median. Training with raw-scale MSE is therefore highly sensitive to a small number of extreme storm hours, motivating the use of a log-transformed target and the reporting of a county-averaged RMSE.

\paragraph{Storm structure.}
Outages are concentrated in a small number of discrete events rather than being uniformly distributed over time. Using a state-wide threshold of 2{,}000 simultaneous outages with a 6-hour minimum duration and a 12-hour merge gap, we identify 26 storm events within the 90-day window (Fig.~\ref{fig:state}). The terminal event (2023-06-23 to 06-30) peaks at 86{,}313, more than three times the second-largest at 27{,}643, and training ends inside it. A single holdout window at the end of training would therefore yield a one-sample estimate dominated by this outlier, motivating the rolling-origin cross-validation design in \S\ref{sec:cv}.

\paragraph{Autocorrelation.}
The state-wide series exhibits a strong diurnal pattern at lag 24h and a
weaker weekly echo at lag 168h (Fig.~\ref{fig:acf}), suggesting that
lagged outage values are likely to dominate prediction, with weather features playing a secondary role.

\paragraph{County-level heterogeneity.}
Per-county \texttt{tracked} values range from approximately 5{,}000 to 920{,}000, and storm impacts do not scale proportionally with size (Fig.~\ref{fig:het}). Wayne County (FIPS 26163) tracks 922{,}000 households yet records only the third-largest peak outage in the state, while smaller counties such as Saginaw (97{,}000 tracked) experience peaks of 8{,}700 outages, roughly 9\% of its household base. This pattern motivates the marginal-benefit allocation in \S\ref{sec:policy} rather than a simple top-$N$ ranking.

\paragraph{Weather features.}
Of the 109 atmospheric variables, 18 are constant over the training period and are therefore dropped, leaving 91 non-zero-variance features. The 15 most correlated with state-wide outages are all moisture- and wind-related, including \texttt{cape}, low-level $u/v$ winds, precipitable water, and storm-motion magnitude (\texttt{vstm}). We retain all 91 features and rely on tree-based models to perform implicit feature selection.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Validation: Rolling-Origin Cross-Validation}
\label{sec:cv}

\paragraph{Motivation.} A naive last-48h holdout falls within the largest storm of the year, so any single-window validation RMSE constitutes a sample of one. We therefore replace it with rolling-origin cross-validation (Fig.~\ref{fig:cv_diag}) consisting of 6 folds spaced 72 hours apart. 

\paragraph{Fold Construction.} The validation window of each fold is the forecast horizon (24 or 48 hours) immediately following its fit window, in exact correspondence with the production setup. The fit windows expand incrementally: each fold uses all data from 2023-04-01 to its origin, so the fit set of fold 0 coincides with the final retrain on the full training data, which makes its RMSE the most direct estimate of test performance [4]. No weather observation inside any validation window is visible to its model fitted on that fold. The folds span 2023-06-13 to 06-29, and exactly one of them (fold 1) lands within the peak of the major storm. 

\paragraph{Reported Metrics.}We report both the aggregate-mean RMSE and the calm-period mean RMSE: the former captures cross-period performance, including the OOD storm, while the latter captures typical-day quality without a single extreme event dominating the score.

\subsection{Feature Engineering}
\label{sec:features}

\paragraph{Feature Matrix Construction.}
For tree-based models, we materialize an $(N\times P)$ feature matrix in which each row corresponds to one (origin time, county, horizon-step) triplet. All features are leak-free in that they use only data available up to time $t$.

\paragraph{Feature Categories.}
Outage lags $y_{c,t-k}$ are computed for $k\in\{1,2,3,6,12,24,48,168\}$, and we add the rolling mean and standard deviation of outage over windows of 6, 24, and 168 hours. The storm indicators include the 24h maximum, the 24h non-zero count, and the 24h day-over-day delta. Weather features enter both at $t$ and at lags of 3, 12, and 24 hours. The calendar encodings (sin/cos of hour, day-of-week, and month) are evaluated at the target time $t{+}h$. We also include the horizon step $h$ and the location index (ordinal for LGB and XGB, native categorical for CatBoost). The final feature dimension is approximately 389. 

\paragraph{Extended Feature Set and PCA.}
We additionally evaluated an enlarged v2 feature set (deep lags up to 672h, state-aggregated lags, county metadata, and weather rolling means; $P\!\approx\!773$), but its performance was within noise of the shorter set (\S\ref{sec:negative}). We considered applying PCA to the 109 weather features but did not adopt it for the headline pipeline. GBDT models perform implicit feature selection through information-gain splits. A linear orthogonal basis would therefore forfeit the per-feature interpretability provided by gain-based importances without delivering a measurable RMSE reduction. We confirmed this empirically: gain-based feature importance in LightGBM assigns 92\% of the total gain to only 23 weather features out of 91, which suggests that the model already exploits a sparse subset.

\subsection{Model Families}

\paragraph{Baselines.}
The naive baselines comprise an all-zero predictor, persistence ($\hat y_{t+h}\!=\!y_t$), seasonal-naive predictors at 24h and 168h, and a per-(county, hour, day-of-week) historical mean. The classical time-series baselines are per-county SARIMAX(1,0,1), the second mandatory baseline of the project, and AutoARIMA over $(p,d,q)$. 

\paragraph{Tabular GBDT Pool.}
The tabular GBDT pool covers LightGBM~[1] (with a log target), LGB-Tweedie~[5], XGBoost~[2], CatBoost~[3] with native categorical handling of FIPS, and Quantile-LGB at $\alpha\!\in\!\{0.5,0.9\}$. We also evaluate a hurdle (zero-inflated) decomposition~[6], formed by a classifier $\Pr[y\!>\!0]$ multiplied by a positive-only regressor, as well as per-county models in which 83 independent regressors are fitted (LGB, XGB, CatBoost). 

\paragraph{Sequence Neural Networks.}
The pool of sequence neural networks covers GRU, LSTM, BiLSTM, TCN, MLP, the vanilla Transformer, NLinear and DLinear~[7], PatchTST~[8], iTransformer~[9], N-BEATS~[10], and N-HiTS~[11]. 

We submit an equal-weight mean ensemble of the top-5 CV models per horizon. Learned stacking weights are not used, since they would over-fit the single-fold storm signal.

\subsection{Hyperparameter Search and Ensemble Selection}

\paragraph{Search Protocol.}
We conducted hyperparameter sweeps on a CMU DeltaAI cluster (NVIDIA H200), which yielded approximately 3{,}800 (model, seed, horizon, fold) cells in total. The LightGBM grid contributed 54 configurations over leaves~$\in\{31,63,127\}$, lr~$\in\{0.03,0.05,0.1\}$, $n\in\{400,800,1500\}$, and subsample~$\in\{0.7,0.9\}$. LGB-Tweedie added 24 configurations with variance\_power $\in\{1.2,1.5,1.8\}$; XGBoost contributed 18 and CatBoost a further 18. Each of the 12 NN architectures was evaluated with 8 hyperparameter combinations and 2 random seeds. Eight further configurations (AutoARIMA, Quantile-LGB, hurdle-LGB and hurdle-XGB, and per-county models) round out the pool. 

\paragraph{Ensemble Construction.}
For ensembling, we compare four combination strategies (mean, median, trimmed mean, and inverse-RMSE-weighted) on the cached fold predictions and select the strategy with the lowest RMSE for each horizon. Top-5 selection ranks models by calm-period mean RMSE, with a cap of two configurations per family, so that the ensemble is not dominated by near-duplicates from a single tuning sweep.

\subsection{Headline: GBDT Ensembles Beat Both Required Baselines}
Tables~\ref{tab:headline24}--\ref{tab:headline48} compare the best
single model to the all-zero baseline and to SARIMAX(1,0,1) under four
aggregation strategies. The improvement is consistent at 7--11\% over
all-zero and 2--6\% over SARIMAX on the 24-hour horizon, with similar
margins on the 48h calm-period score. Storm fold 1 contributes RMSE in
the 700--900 range for every model evaluated, including SARIMAX, all
100+ tree configurations, and all SOTA Transformer variants, so its
absolute contribution is large in unit terms but small in relative terms.

\paragraph{Ensemble vs.\ single best.}
The submission consists of the equal-weight mean of the top-5 models
per horizon (see \S\ref{sec:methods}.4). The 24h ensemble combines
\texttt{xgb\_pca20}, \texttt{cat\_v2}, \texttt{lgb\_pca20},
\texttt{cat\_d8\_lr03\_n400}, and \texttt{lgb\_quantile\_p50}; the 48h
ensemble combines \texttt{lgb\_pca30}, \texttt{xgb\_pca20},
\texttt{lgb\_pca20}, \texttt{cat\_v2}, and \texttt{cat\_pca20}. On the
cached fold-level predictions, the 24h ensemble achieves a calm-period
RMSE of 41.17, only 0.08 below the best single model (\texttt{xgb\_pca20}
at 41.25); the 48h ensemble lands at 44.37 against 44.54 for the best
single model ($\Delta\!=\!0.17$). The marginal improvement reflects the
high prediction correlation among GBDT variants, so the value of the
ensemble lies in robustness against single-configuration failure (for
example, a particular hyperparameter combination that overfits one
sub-region of the feature space) rather than in raw RMSE reduction. On
storm fold 1, the best single model slightly outperforms the ensemble
(24h: 840 vs.\ 859; 48h: 715 vs.\ 718), which suggests that averaging
GBDT predictions does not help for OOD events: the single model that
happens to commit to the correct magnitude wins, and averaging dilutes
that commitment. We submit the ensemble nonetheless, partly because the
project framing is a single-fold-CSV evaluation in which calm-period
behaviour dominates, and partly because the choice follows the
M4-competition consensus that ensemble averaging is the most robust
forecasting practice [13].

\subsection{Per-Family Comparison and Per-Fold Breakdown}
Figure~\ref{fig:per_family} reports the best configuration of each
model family on the 24h horizon. On calm-period RMSE, GBDT variants
cluster tightly between 41.5 and 43.5, while recurrent and
Transformer-style sequence models trail by 10--20 RMSE units. This
pattern reproduces the finding of [7]: on short, multivariate,
autocorrelated series, simpler inductive biases (boosted trees)
outperform self-attention. On the storm fold all families collapse to
RMSE 700--900, with the ranking effectively randomised, which confirms
that storm-event extrapolation is bounded by data rather than by
architecture.

Figure~\ref{fig:family_fold} disaggregates this finding by reporting
the best configuration of each family on every fold. The GBDT cluster
(top 13 rows) is visibly homogeneous; the sequence-NN families
consistently lose 10--30 RMSE on calm folds; fold 0 (the fold
immediately adjacent to the test horizon) is the most difficult calm
fold across all families, with RMSE in the 70--90 range against 19--44
elsewhere.

\subsection{What Did Not Improve the Result}
\label{sec:negative}
Several priors did not move the CV RMSE below the GBDT cluster.
Doubling the feature count (the v2 set) was within noise of the
390-feature baseline. Direct MSE, Huber, and sample-weighted objectives
on the raw outage scale degraded fold-0 calm RMSE from 71 to 73--146,
which reflects the heavy-tail sensitivity to log-space training. A
recursive (autoregressive) one-step rollout accumulated error through
24--48 iterations and was strictly worse than the direct multi-output
formulation that we adopted. None of the NN architectures we tried
(GRU, LSTM, BiLSTM, TCN, Transformer, NLinear, DLinear, PatchTST,
iTransformer, N-BEATS, and N-HiTS) closed the 10+ RMSE gap with GBDT
on calm folds. A diagnostic per-county decomposition reveals that 50\%
of the fold-0 RMSE is concentrated in Wayne County alone, where the
truth peaks at 2{,}648 against a prediction of 536. This
unfolding-storm underestimation persists across every variant tested,
and we therefore interpret RMSE 41--42 as the achievable lower bound
for this dataset under the no-future-weather constraint.

\section{Analysis and Policy Recommendation}
\label{sec:policy}

\subsection{From Forecast to Action: Decision-Maker Perspective}
A point forecast $\hat y_{c,t}$ does not yet constitute a decision.
From the vantage point of the utility operator, the question is
concrete: given the predicted outage geography, where should the
mobile generators be staged tonight so that they will absorb the
maximum number of household-hours of outage tomorrow? The instrument
is subject to hard physical constraints, since each of the five mobile
generators serves at most 1{,}000 households at any moment and, once
deployed, cannot be relocated during the 48-hour event window. The
decision is taken before the event, with the same information set used
by the model (history and weather observed up to the forecast origin),
so the recommendation constitutes an honest test of forecast-driven
proactive action.

\subsection{Why This Set Rather than Naive Heuristics}
Each of four naive heuristics fails in a manner that is corrected by
our forecast-driven greedy algorithm. A top-5 historical-peak list
(Wayne, Oakland, Macomb, Genesee, Kent) treats outage as a static
county property and ignores the storm track. A top-5 ranking by tracked
households produces the same metro list and ignores per-capita risk:
Saginaw (97k tracked) sustained 8.7k outage in the June storm, which
corresponds to 9\% of its base, far more than any of the metros. A
top-5 ranking by predicted 48h sum double-counts long, low-amplitude
outage; the per-hour cap $\min(\hat y, 1000)$ inside $M_s$ corrects
this. A one-generator-per-county rule forbids stacking, whereas the
greedy algorithm correctly places multiple generators where the
predicted demand is well above 1{,}000 per hour, which the project
description explicitly permits.

\subsection{Sensitivity, Robustness, and Decision Risk}
Selection robustness. When the top-5 are re-derived from each ensemble
member, the first three counties are picked unanimously, and counties
4 and 5 vary only marginally. Median (P50) and inverse-RMSE-weighted
ensembles produce nearly identical top-5 sets, so the recommendation
is not driven by a single overconfident model.

Decision risk against the real outcome. The recommendation will be
evaluated against the actual outage record from 2023-06-30 to 07-02.
Three sources of risk could erode the ex-post served-household-hours.
The unfolding storm could intensify in counties under-predicted by the
model (Wayne in particular, where the fold-0 truth peak of 2{,}648
against a prediction of 536 identifies a systematic gap). The storm
could clear earlier than expected, in which case the 48-hour staging
would over-commit. A previously-calm county could experience a flash
event that is invisible to a model without future-weather input. We
mitigate the first risk by cross-checking the top-5 list of the
pessimistic Quantile-LGB-$P_{90}$ forecast (the overlap with the
mean-forecast top-5 is the robustness metric reported above). The
second and third are residual risks inherent to the 90-day training
window and the no-future-weather constraint.

Limitations. The training data span 90 days within a single
spring-to-summer window, so the model has not seen winter ice storms
or fall hurricane remnants, which makes year-on-year retraining
essential in deployment. The absence of future-weather information
caps the 48h predictability of fast convective events; the integration
of an NWP forecast at inference time would substantially improve both
prediction and policy quality, but it falls outside the framing of
this project. Finally, the recommendation is derived from a point
forecast, and for richer products such as uncertainty bands or
regret-aware allocation, the Quantile-LGB-$P_{90}$ member is available
in our pool.
