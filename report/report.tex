\section{Introduction}
Extreme-weather-driven power outages are a recurring societal cost in the
US Midwest. We are given hourly outage counts and 109 atmospheric/hydrological
features for the 83 Michigan counties over a 90-day window
(\textbf{2023-04-01 to 2023-06-30}), and asked to (i) forecast outages over
the next 24 and 48 hours and (ii) recommend the placement of five 1{,}000-household
backup generators. Two design constraints shape the entire pipeline:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{No future weather is available at test time.} Only outage history
plus weather observed up to and including the forecast origin can be used.
\item \textbf{The training window ends mid-storm.} The largest event of the
year (peak 86{,}313 outages on 2023-06-29) starts on 2023-06-23 and runs
through the end of training. The test horizon begins one hour after the
final training observation, so any model has to extrapolate \emph{into} a
storm without ground-truth weather.
\end{itemize}
These constraints directly motivate our validation design (\S\ref{sec:cv})
and feature engineering (\S\ref{sec:features}).

\section{Data Analysis}
\label{sec:eda}
The data is a single \texttt{.nc} file with shape $T{=}2{,}161 \times L{=}83 \times F{=}109$
plus an outage matrix $\mathbf{Y}\in\mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}^{T\times L}$ and a per-county
\texttt{tracked} household count.

\paragraph{Sparse, heavy-tailed outage counts.}
70.5\% of $(c,t)$ cells have zero outages, and the non-zero distribution is
heavy-tailed (Fig.~\ref{fig:dist}). Across the full window, the state-wide hourly
outage total ranges from 105 to 86{,}313, with the 99.5th percentile already
ten times the median --- a \textbf{zero-inflated, log-normal-tailed regression
target}.

\paragraph{Storm-event structure.}
Using a state-wide threshold of 2{,}000 simultaneous outages with a 6-hour minimum
duration (12-hour merge gap), we detect \textbf{26 distinct storm events}
in the 90-day window (Fig.~\ref{fig:state}). The largest event (id 25,
2023-06-23 -- 2023-06-30) has a peak of 86{,}313, more than 3$\times$ the
second-largest (id 0, 27{,}643). The training window ends inside this event.

\paragraph{Strong autocorrelation.}
The state-wide outage series is highly autocorrelated, with a clear
diurnal echo at lag 24h and a weaker weekly echo at lag 168h
(Fig.~\ref{fig:acf}). This implies that lagged outage counts should
dominate any feature set; weather is informative but secondary because
its predictive value is partly already encoded in past outages.

\paragraph{County heterogeneity.}
Per-county \texttt{tracked} (the maximum number of households tracked) ranges
from $\sim$5{,}000 to over 920{,}000. Wayne County (FIPS 26163, Detroit)
peaks at 922{,}425 households tracked but its peak hourly outage of 15{,}143
is only the third largest, while Genesee (26049) and Macomb (26099) take
disproportionate hits per household (Fig.~\ref{fig:het}). This heterogeneity
later drives our policy recommendation (\S\ref{sec:policy}).

\paragraph{Top weather correlates.}
The 15 weather features most correlated with state-wide outage are
all moisture-and-wind-related (\texttt{cape}, low-level $u/v$ winds,
precipitable water, \texttt{vstm} storm-motion magnitude). We retain all
non-zero-variance features (91 after filtering); tree models implicitly
select among them.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Validation: Rolling-Origin CV that Mirrors the Test Setup}
\label{sec:cv}
A naive last-48h holdout falls inside the largest storm of the year, so any
single-window val-RMSE is a sample of one and dominated by storm-period error.
We replace it with a \textbf{rolling-origin cross-validation} scheme
(Fig.~\ref{fig:cv_diag}) consisting of 6 folds spaced 72 hours apart.
\begin{itemize}
\item Each fold's validation window equals the forecast horizon (24 or 48 hours)
\emph{immediately following} its fit window --- exactly the production setup.
\item No weather observations from inside any val window are visible to its model.
\item Folds span 2023-06-13 through 2023-06-29; one of them (fold 1) lands in the
big-storm peak. Reporting mean$\pm$std across folds prevents a single high-leverage
fold from determining the model ranking.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Feature Engineering}
\label{sec:features}
For tree-based and linear models we materialize a $(N\times P)$ feature
matrix in which each row is one (origin time, county, horizon-step) triplet.
Features (all leak-free, using only data up to time $t$):
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=12pt]
\item \textbf{Outage lags} --- $y_{c,t-k}$ for $k\in\{1,2,3,6,12,24,48,168\}$.
\item \textbf{Outage rolling stats} --- mean and std over $\{6,24,168\}$-hour windows.
\item \textbf{Storm indicators} --- rolling 24h max (peak intensity), 24h
 non-zero count (storm duration), $y_{c,t}-y_{c,t-24}$ (day-over-day delta).
\item \textbf{Weather at $t$ and lags $\{3,12,24\}$h} --- captures pre-storm build-up.
\item \textbf{Calendar at target time $t+h$} --- $\sin/\cos$ of hour-of-day,
 day-of-week, month-of-year ($6$ features).
\item \textbf{Horizon step $h$} --- so a single global model can output all 48 steps.
\item \textbf{Location index} --- ordinal for LightGBM/XGBoost, native categorical
 for CatBoost.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Model Families}
We benchmark a deliberately broad set of models so that the report can defend
each modeling choice with a baseline.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=12pt]
\item \textbf{Naive baselines (deterministic)}: all-zero, persistence
($\hat y_{t+h} = y_t$), seasonal-naive-24h, seasonal-naive-168h, and a
per-(county,hour-of-day,day-of-week) historical mean.
\item \textbf{Classical TS}: per-county SARIMAX(1,0,1), the second
mandatory baseline named in the project description.
\item \textbf{Tabular gradient-boosted trees}: LightGBM (log-target), LightGBM with
Tweedie loss (compound Poisson--Gamma, designed for zero-inflated positive
data), XGBoost, CatBoost (native categorical FIPS handling).
\item \textbf{Hurdle/zero-inflated decomposition}: a binary classifier predicts
$\Pr[y>0]$; a regressor trained on positive rows only predicts the magnitude;
the combined estimator is $\hat y = \Pr[y>0]\cdot\hat y_{>0}$.
\item \textbf{Per-county LightGBM}: 83 independent gradient-boosted trees,
one per county, exploiting heterogeneity.
\item \textbf{Sequence models}: GRU, BiLSTM, DLinear, PatchTST (run on H200).
\item \textbf{Equal-weight ensemble}: arithmetic mean of the top-$k$ CV models.
We deliberately avoid \emph{learned} stacking weights because all weight
training would happen on the same val folds and would over-fit the
big-storm fold.
\end{itemize}

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\paragraph{Rolling-origin CV summary.}
Table~\ref{tab:cv24} reports CV RMSE for the 24h horizon; Table~\ref{tab:cv48}
the 48h. Both tables show the mean$\pm$std across 6 folds (one seed per
stochastic model in this iteration). Two patterns are immediately visible:

\textbf{(1) The mean is dominated by fold 1 (the storm peak.)}
For every model the per-fold maximum RMSE (achieved on fold 1) is one to two
orders of magnitude larger than the minimum (fold 4, a quiet 48h window).
The fold-level heatmap (Fig.~\ref{fig:heatmap}) makes this concrete.
This is exactly the symptom we expected from an OOD-storm test setup, and
it explains why simple baselines like \emph{persistence} score very
competitively on mean-RMSE.

\textbf{(2) LightGBM beats SARIMAX on 24h and ties on 48h.}
At 24h, LightGBM averages \textbf{172.8} RMSE vs.\ SARIMAX's \textbf{179.9}
and the all-zero baseline's \textbf{189.5}.
At 48h, LightGBM (158.8) and SARIMAX (157.9) are within noise, both better
than all-zero (160.9). The differential reflects LightGBM's stronger
near-term autoregressive signal vs.\ SARIMAX's better-behaved long-horizon
mean-reversion.

\section{Analysis and Policy Recommendation}
\label{sec:policy}

\subsection{From forecast to action}
A point forecast $\hat y_{c,t}$ is not yet a decision; the utility needs an
allocation $\pi:\{\text{generators}\}\to\{\text{counties}\}$ that maximizes
expected mitigated outage. Each generator covers up to 1{,}000 households for
the entire 48-hour test horizon, and once placed it cannot be relocated.

\subsection{Why we pick this set, and not the obvious alternatives}
A naive analyst might propose four heuristics. Each has a defect that the
ensemble forecast addresses.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=14pt]
\item \textbf{``Pick the 5 counties with the highest historical peak.''}
This collapses to Wayne, Oakland, Macomb, Genesee, Kent — the five biggest
metros — regardless of whether a storm is coming. It treats outage
frequency as a static county property, but the test window's outage
geography is determined by the storm's track. Our forecast incorporates
the storm-period weather build-up encoded in the lag features.

\item \textbf{``Pick the 5 counties with the most tracked households.''}
Same answer, slightly different reasoning. But Saginaw (FIPS 26145) has only
97k tracked yet hit 8.7k peak outage in the big June storm — 9\% of its
households at once, far more than the metros. Per-capita risk matters.

\item \textbf{``Pick the 5 counties with the highest predicted 48h sum.''}
This double-counts long-but-mild outage in highly-tracked counties.
Wayne's continuous low-amplitude background dominates the integral even
when no single hour exceeds 1{,}000.
Our greedy criterion uses
$\min(\hat y, 1000)$ — we cap the per-hour benefit at 1 generator's
capacity — which correctly de-emphasizes counties whose outage already
exceeds what one generator can serve.

\item \textbf{``Distribute 1 generator each to 5 different counties.''}
The greedy doesn't enforce uniqueness; if a county's predicted peak
$\sum_{h}\min(\hat y, 1000)$ remains the largest after deducting one
generator's capacity, the algorithm will (and should) place a second one
there. This is a feature, not a bug — it matches the project description
allowing repeated entries.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Sensitivity and robustness}
\paragraph{Forecast horizon.}
Comparing 24-h vs.\ 48-h forecasts: if the test window is dominated by a
single storm passage, both horizons recommend the same set; if the storm
clears in the first 24 hours, the 48-h recommendation may shift toward
counties with later-onset outage. We provide the recommendation derived
from the 48-h forecast as the primary answer because the test horizon is 48
hours; the 24-h derived list is reported in the appendix.

\paragraph{Forecast uncertainty.}
Our ensemble is a point forecast. To quantify selection robustness we
re-derive the top-5 from each ensemble member separately (LightGBM, the
top-ranked Transformer, etc.). The first three counties are picked
unanimously; counties 4 and 5 vary across the ensemble — these are the
margins where additional weather information would most change the policy.

\paragraph{Alternative loss for the ensemble.}
Median (P50) and inverse-RMSE-weighted ensembles produce nearly identical
top-5 selections, suggesting the recommendation is not driven by extrapolation
from a single overconfident model. The full strategy comparison appears in
\texttt{report\_assets/ensemble\_meta.json}.

\paragraph{Limitations of this design.}
\textbf{(i) 90 days of training data, all spring-summer.} Our model has
literally never seen a winter ice-storm or a fall hurricane remnant.
\textbf{(ii) No future weather at test time.} A 48-hour weather forecast
would substantially improve the policy decision but the project framing
forbids it. \textbf{(iii) Point forecasts only.} The Quantile-LGB-P90 model
included in our pool would, if surfaced separately, justify pre-positioning
extra generators in the worst-case-outage counties — a richer policy product
that we leave for future work.
